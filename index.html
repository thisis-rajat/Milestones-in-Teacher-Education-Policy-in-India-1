Milestones in Teacher Education Policy in India

Munro‚Äôs Teacher Training Proposal

As early as 1826, Sir Thomas Munro, then Governor of Madras, recognized the need for trained teachers in India‚Äôs schools. He proposed establishing teacher training schools in each district (collectorate) to supply qualified ‚Äúnative teachers‚Äù for the expanding school system.

üí° Key Points:
  • Munro‚Äôs plan recommended government support (funding and stipends) for training local candidates as teachers.
  • This included opening special schools to prepare teachers, rather than importing teachers from England, thus laying groundwork for an indigenous teacher education system.
üìà Impact:
  • Munro‚Äôs initiative led to early funding for teacher training by the colonial administration. Though limited in scope, it set a precedent for formal teacher education in India, influencing later policies like Wood‚Äôs Despatch (1854) that institutionalized teacher training across presidencies.

Wood‚Äôs Despatch (Magna Carta of Indian Education)

Sir Charles Wood‚Äôs Despatch of 1854 was a landmark policy for education in British India. It devoted special attention to teacher education as the linchpin of quality schooling, earning the nickname ‚ÄúMagna Charta of English Education in India.‚Äù

üí° Key Points:
  • Wood‚Äôs Despatch advocated establishing dedicated normal schools in each province to train teachers.
  • It introduced a pupil-teacher system (apprenticeship model) where aspiring teachers (pupil-teachers) would receive stipends and hands-on training under experienced masters.
  • It also recommended certificates and employment guarantees for those who completed training, incentivizing the teaching profession.
üìà Impact:
  • The Despatch led to the founding of teacher training institutions across India‚Äôs presidencies.
  • By 1882, 106 normal schools were in operation (including some exclusively for women).
  • The policy firmly established teacher training as a responsibility of the state, creating a cadre of locally trained teachers and setting standards, such as requiring a teaching certificate for salary grants to schools, that professionalized teaching in India.

Lord Stanley‚Äôs Despatch

In 1859, Sir Edward Stanley (Secretary of State for India) issued a despatch reinforcing the importance of indigenous teacher training. This policy built on Wood‚Äôs Despatch by further discouraging reliance on England for teachers and solidifying local training efforts.

üí° Key Points:
  • Lord Stanley‚Äôs Despatch insisted that teachers for Indian schools should be trained locally (particularly for vernacular schools) rather than imported.
  • It tied government grants to teacher qualifications: only schools with certified trained teachers were eligible for certain salary grants.
  • This created a clear incentive for schools to have trained staff.
üìà Impact:
  • The 1859 Despatch gave a strong impetus to teacher-training programs in India.
  • The linkage of grants to trained teachers meant a rapid increase in demand for teacher education.
  • By the early 1880s, the policy had helped expand the network of normal schools and firmly established the norm that teaching positions required professional training.

Hunter Commission (Indian Education Commission)

The Indian Education Commission of 1882, popularly known as the Hunter Commission, was the first comprehensive review of education in India. It recognized that improving teacher training was crucial for elevating educational standards.

üí° Key Points:
  • The Hunter Commission authorized and formalized distinct training programs for elementary and secondary teachers.
  • It recommended creating a separate secondary teacher training program with rigorous examinations in the principles and practice of teaching.
  • Passing this exam was made mandatory for permanent appointment as a teacher in any government or aided secondary school.
üìà Impact:
  • Acting on these recommendations, the late 19th century saw the establishment of six new stand-alone teacher training colleges.
  • These colleges awarded a Licentiate in Teaching (LT) diploma, equivalent to a degree.
  • Additionally, about 50 training schools for secondary teachers emerged.
  • By century‚Äôs end, India had a diversified institutional framework for teacher education ‚Äì normal schools for primary teachers, secondary training schools, and colleges ‚Äì in both government and missionary/private sectors.
  • The Hunter Commission‚Äôs push for qualified teachers greatly professionalized teaching and laid the foundation for a structured teacher education system.

Government of India Education Policy Resolution (Curzon‚Äôs Reforms)

Lord Curzon‚Äôs 1904 resolution on education policy aimed to improve quality at all levels of education, with teacher training as a central focus. Curzon saw poorly trained teachers as a cause of low educational standards and sought to standardize and modernize teacher education.

üí° Key Points:
  • The 1904 resolution set standards for recognizing schools, including mandates on teacher qualifications and training facilities.
  • It recommended strengthening training colleges for secondary teachers by equipping them with better facilities and linking each college with a practicing model school.
  • The duration of training was specified: one year for graduates and two years for undergraduates entering teaching.
  • Training curricula were to tightly integrate educational theory with practical teaching skills.
  • For primary teacher training, it called for expanding normal schools (especially in Bengal) and set a two-year minimum course focused on preparing teachers for rural schools.
üìà Impact:
  • Curzon‚Äôs reforms led to qualitative improvements in teacher preparation.
  • Training colleges and schools upgraded their infrastructure and curriculum to meet the new norms.
  • The insistence on practice teaching (through attached model schools) professionalized the practicum component of teacher education.
  • Overall, the 1904 resolution helped create a more systematic and quality-oriented teacher training regime in India, directly linking teacher qualifications to school recognition and funding.

Education Policy Resolution

The Government of India‚Äôs 1913 resolution on education policy further emphasized teacher qualifications as non-negotiable for modern schooling. It underscored that no teacher should be allowed to teach without proper training and certification.

üí° Key Points:
  • The resolution proposed that teachers be recruited from the same social class/community as their students (to ensure cultural connect), but only if they had at least a middle school (vernacular) pass and one year of formal teacher training.
  • It stressed periodic revision of teacher training curricula.
  • Another innovative suggestion was fostering professional learning communities: teacher-training college staff were urged to regularly exchange ideas and visit each other‚Äôs institutions.
üìà Impact:
  • This policy reinforced the principle that teaching is a skilled profession requiring certification.
  • Provinces began enforcing qualification requirements more strictly.
  • The emphasis on curriculum renewal and inter-college exchanges sowed the seeds for a more dynamic teacher education culture.
  • It paved the way for a more unified standard in teacher training across British India.

Calcutta University Commission (Sadler Commission)

Chaired by Dr. Michael Sadler, this commission primarily reviewed higher education, but it also made significant recommendations for teacher education as part of the university system.

üí° Key Points:
  • The Sadler Commission suggested that universities should take an active role in teacher training.
  • It recommended establishing Departments of Education at universities (such as Dacca and Calcutta) and introducing Education as an optional subject at the Intermediate, B.A., and M.A. levels.
  • This meant prospective teachers could study education academically during their college years, integrating content knowledge with pedagogical theory.
üìà Impact:
  • The Commission‚Äôs ideas had a positive impact on teacher education programs.
  • Following its report, universities started engaging in teacher preparation. For example, Mysore University established a Faculty of Education in 1925.
  • By bringing teacher education into mainstream universities, the Sadler Commission helped elevate the intellectual and professional standards of teacher training, influencing later moves to integrate teacher education with higher education institutions.

Nation-Building and Basic Education (Gandhian initiatives)

The 1920s saw indigenous movements which indirectly shaped teacher development. Mahatma Gandhi‚Äôs call for Nai Talim (Basic Education) in 1937‚Äì38 introduced a radically different approach requiring new teacher training paradigms. Though not a government commission, the Wardha Conference (1937) and the Zakir Husain Committee (1938) on basic education were key Indian-led initiatives influencing TPD.

üí° Key Points:
  • Basic Education envisioned schooling up to grade 8 centered on handicrafts, self-sufficiency, and holistic development.
  • To implement this, teachers needed training in craft-based, experiential learning methods.
  • Jamia Millia Islamia in Delhi took up the challenge ‚Äì in 1938 it established a Teachers‚Äô College for Basic Education, arguably the first program to train teachers in learning-by-doing methods.
  • Similar training centers were set up at Wardha (Maharashtra) and Gandhigram (Tamil Nadu).
  • Curricula for these training programs stressed craft skills, integration of work and curriculum, use of mother tongue, and development of social values.
üìà Impact:
  • These efforts were the earliest attempts to ‚Äúindigenize‚Äù teacher education, aligning it with Indian realities and nationalist aspirations.
  • They demonstrated that teachers could be trained to deliver a radically different curriculum focused on self-reliance and social reconstruction.
  • Although basic education did not scale nationwide under colonial rule, it influenced post-independence thinking on work-experience in curriculum and showed the importance of preparing teachers for the local context.

Hartog Committee Report

The Hartog Committee (1929) was appointed to examine problems of education (notably the high dropout rate in primary schools) in the wake of rapid expansion of schools during the 1920s. While focused on primary education quality, it inevitably addressed teacher training.

üí° Key Points:
  • For primary teacher training, Hartog recommended extending the duration of training programs (to impart deeper skills) and ensuring training institutes were adequately staffed.
  • Importantly, it highlighted that improving teachers‚Äô service conditions (salary, career prospects) was essential to attract and retain talent.
  • Hartog also suggested selecting primary teachers from local rural communities.
üìà Impact:
  • These recommendations led to concrete measures.
  • In the 1930s, provincial governments initiated in-service training programs for primary teachers.
  • The Central Advisory Board of Education (CABE) adopted Hartog‚Äôs guidelines in 1943, standardizing teacher training durations: 2 years for pre-primary and primary, 3 years for middle-school, etc.
  • This brought uniformity to teacher education across India.
  • The emphasis on in-service education acknowledged that learning for teachers doesn‚Äôt stop at initial training ‚Äì a principle that would gain prominence in independent India‚Äôs TPD policies.

Abbott-Wood Report

The Abbott-Wood Report (1937) mainly examined vocational education, but it also contributed a forward-looking perspective on teacher education. Authored by British experts, the report‚Äôs insights were ahead of their time in teacher training.

üí° Key Points:
  • Abbott-Wood recommended a three-year teacher training course after high school, combining general higher education with professional training ‚Äì essentially an early call for integrated teacher education programs.
  • The report also introduced the idea of periodic refresher courses for in-service teachers.
üìà Impact:
  • While colonial authorities did not implement a 3-year training nationwide, the concept influenced educational planners.
  • The notion of integrated teacher degrees would resurface in independent India (e.g., the four-year programs in the 1960s and again in NEP 2020).
  • The idea of regular in-service refresher training was validated and later became standard practice in national programs.
  • In essence, the Abbott-Wood Report planted seeds for longer pre-service training and continuous in-service development, recognizing teaching as a career of lifelong learning.

Sargent Plan (Post-war Education Development)

In 1944, the colonial government set up the Sargent Committee (chaired by Sir John Sargent) to envision a 40-year post-WWII plan for Indian education. Teacher training was a prominent part of this ambitious plan.

üí° Key Points:
  • The Sargent Report endorsed the training duration standards previously recommended by Hartog (1929) and adopted by CABE in 1943, reaffirming 2 years for primary teachers, etc.
  • It proposed proactively identifying promising high-school students for the teaching profession and offering them stipends/scholarships.
  • The plan also emphasized regular in-service refresher courses and gave teachers access to research facilities.
üìà Impact:
  • Although the Sargent Plan‚Äôs broader targets remained mostly on paper due to the impending end of British rule, it set a visionary benchmark.
  • Several ideas ‚Äì recruiting teacher candidates early, institutionalizing in-service training, and viewing teaching as a research-informed profession ‚Äì influenced post-independence policies.

University Education Commission (Radhakrishnan Commission)

Post-independence, the University Education Commission (1948‚Äì49), chaired by Dr. S. Radhakrishnan, examined all levels of education. It critiqued the state of teacher preparation, especially the practical training of teachers, as insufficient.

üí° Key Points:
  • The Commission observed that student-teachers were not getting enough school experience (teaching practice).
  • It recommended a minimum of 12 weeks of supervised teaching practice, with a flexible approach to supervision (not needing constant monitor presence).
  • It also cautioned training colleges against admitting more trainees than they could effectively train.
üìà Impact:
  • These recommendations drew attention to the quality (not just quantity) of teacher training.
  • Many training programs began to increase the length and rigor of practice teaching.
  • The call for limiting intake to maintain quality foreshadowed later regulatory efforts by bodies like NCTE.
  • In essence, the Commission‚Äôs focus on practical training and adaptability influenced the reorientation of B.Ed curricula in the 1950s and beyond.

Secondary Education Commission (Mudaliar Commission)

The Secondary Education Commission, led by Dr. A. Lakshmanaswami Mudaliar, devoted a section of its 1953 report to improving teacher education as a means to revitalize secondary schools.

Key Recommendations:
  • Raise the minimum education for entry into primary teacher training to a High School diploma (10th grade), and make the training course two years long.
  • Require secondary teachers to be university graduates and undergo a one-year B.Ed. (with a plan to eventually extend this to two years).
  • Experiment with a four-year integrated teacher training program (combining content and pedagogy) in select institutions as an innovative model. It also detailed a richer practical internship for B.Ed. trainees.
üìà Impact:
  • These suggestions had far-reaching influence.
  • The idea of four-year integrated teacher education took shape: in 1960, Kurukshetra University launched India‚Äôs first 4-year B.A./B.Sc. B.Ed. program.
  • By 1963, all four of NCERT‚Äôs Regional Institutes of Education had implemented four-year integrated courses.
  • The Commission‚Äôs push for comprehensive practical training enriched B.Ed. curricula across the country.

Ford Foundation Team (on Teacher Education)

In 1954, India invited an international team of experts (with Ford Foundation collaboration) to review teacher education, reflecting the importance placed on global best practices.

  • It strengthened the link between theory and practice, enabling real-time feedback for trainee teachers.

Establishment of NCERT and Regional Colleges

The National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT) was established by the Government of India in 1961 as an apex body for education. While not a commission, this institutional development was crucial for teacher training in India.

Key Role in TPD:

NCERT set up Regional Institutes of Education (RIEs) in different zones of India to serve as model teacher education colleges (building on ideas from the 1953 Commission and Ford team). These RIEs pioneered integrated teacher education programs and updated pedagogy. NCERT also absorbed and coordinated existing national research and training efforts, developing model curricula, syllabi, and teaching materials. Through its Department of Teacher Education, NCERT became an advisory leader, conducting in-service teacher training at national scale (e.g., summer institutes, orientation programs) and shaping pre-service teacher education content.

üìà Impact:
  • The formation of NCERT provided India with a dedicated institution to professionalize teacher education.
  • It facilitated consistent national standards and innovations: NCERT‚Äôs curriculum frameworks for schools (starting 1975) and related teacher education frameworks (through the NCTE, which was initially under NCERT) guided states in reforming teacher training.
  • The RIEs served as laboratories for new teacher education models (like the four-year B.Ed., and later two-year B.Ed. experiments), many of which influenced policy.
  • NCERT also conducted research that fed into major commissions (like Kothari Commission) and into training design (such as activity-based learning methods), thereby deeply shaping both in-service and pre-service teacher development across India.

Kothari Commission & National Education Policy 1968

The Education Commission (1964‚Äì66), chaired by Dr. D. S. Kothari, was a landmark comprehensive review of Indian education. It devoted considerable attention to teacher education, calling teachers the ‚Äúnation‚Äôs strength‚Äù and crucial for quality improvement. üí° Key Points:

  • It recommended establishing ‚Äúcomprehensive colleges of education.‚Äù The subsequent NPE 1968 reiterated improving teacher status, better pay, recognition, and career development.

  • Short-term training courses and correspondence teacher training programs were launched, enabling thousands of untrained teachers to get credentials while in service.
  • The idea of comprehensive education colleges influenced the upgrading or creation of institutions that combined pre-service, in-service, and research functions.
  • The NPE 1968 itself set the tone for treating teacher preparation and continual learning as a continuum.

National Policy on Education (NPE) and Programme of Action 1992

NPE 1986 was a sweeping policy that, among other things, made teacher education a priority area. It famously stated that ‚Äúteacher education is a continuous process, and its pre-service and in-service components are inseparable.‚Äù

üí° Key Points:
  • NPE 1986 launched a new centrally sponsored scheme to restructure teacher education.
  • It recommended establishing District Institutes of Education and Training (DIETs) in each district for training elementary school teachers, upgrading teacher training colleges into Colleges of Teacher Education (CTEs), creating Institutes of Advanced Studies in Education (IASEs), and strengthening SCERTs.
  • The 1990 Ramamurti Review suggested an internship model for teacher training and more personalized in-service programs.
üìà Impact:
  • From the late 1980s into the 1990s, DIETs were established across India.
  • Many teacher colleges were upgraded to CTEs/IASEs.
  • This decentralized support structure (DIET-CTE-IASE-SCERT) permanently changed how teacher development was institutionalized.
  • The concept of continuous teacher development took root, with every teacher expected to undergo regular in-service training.

  • NCTE gained the authority to set norms for teacher education courses and infrastructure, grant recognition to TEIs, and shut down substandard colleges.

üìà Impact:
  • The statutory NCTE dramatically reshaped the teacher education landscape.
  • All teacher training institutions had to seek NCTE recognition, leading to closure of many inadequate programs.
  • By establishing minimum duration, content, and resource requirements, it brought uniformity and accountability to teacher training across India.

District Primary Education Programme (DPEP)

Launched in 1994, DPEP was a centrally sponsored program (with World Bank assistance) aimed at revitalizing primary education in select districts across 7 states. A cornerstone of DPEP was teacher empowerment and training as the key to improving classroom processes.

üîß Key Features:
  • DPEP introduced a decentralized cascade model for in-service teacher development.
  • It established Block Resource Centres (BRCs) and Cluster Resource Centres (CRCs) for ongoing training and support.
  • Regular CRC meetings and school visits helped provide onsite guidance and mentorship.
  • Included both short (10‚Äì20 day) pedagogy training and longer (30‚Äì60 day) courses for undertrained teachers.
  • Promoted child-centered teaching, multi-grade strategies, and new learning materials.
  • Supported teacher qualification upgrades via distance education.
üìà Impact:
  • Though initially limited to about 42 districts, DPEP‚Äôs model had national influence.
  • The BRC/CRC framework proved effective and was later scaled up nationwide under Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan in 2001.
  • Teachers in DPEP areas experienced more continuous professional development through regular cluster meetings and improved classroom practices as reported by evaluations.
  • DPEP also demonstrated successful innovations (like school-based mentoring, teacher-developed teaching aids, and sharing of best practices) which informed subsequent policy.
  • In summary, DPEP significantly shifted the paradigm of in-service teacher training from infrequent centralized workshops to ongoing decentralized support, and its legacy continues in India‚Äôs teacher support systems.

Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan (SSA)

SSA, launched in 2001, was India‚Äôs flagship program to achieve Universal Elementary Education. Teacher professional development was one of SSA‚Äôs critical components, building on the lessons of DPEP to improve the quality of education while expanding access.

üöÄ Key Initiatives:
  • Mandated 20 days of in-service training per teacher per year on pedagogy, content, inclusion, etc.
  • Bridge courses (30‚Äì60 days) for untrained in-service teachers and induction training for new recruits.
  • Institutionalized BRC/CRC support system for monthly workshops, demo classes, and school visits.
  • Scaled innovative models (e.g., Nali Kali, ABL) to other states.
  • Community engagement through SMC training to strengthen school accountability and teacher participation.
üìà Impact:
  • Under SSA, millions of teachers received regular training, something unprecedented in scale.
  • By decentralizing training to sub-district levels, SSA helped contextualize teacher development ‚Äì trainers could address local language issues or specific learning gaps.
  • The period 2003‚Äì2010 saw a flurry of activity-based and child-centric methodologies enter classrooms as teachers were trained in new approaches aligned with National Curriculum Framework 2005.
  • However, the outcomes were mixed: while teacher attendance in training was high, multiple assessments (like ASER, etc.) indicated that learning levels did not rise commensurately.
  • Nonetheless, SSA permanently changed the TPD landscape: continuous in-service teacher training and onsite support became an expected norm.
  • Even after SSA merged into the broader Samagra Shiksha in 2018, the structures and practices it established (BRC/CRC system, annual teacher training budgets, etc.) remain the backbone of in-service teacher professional development in India.

National Curriculum Framework (NCF 2005)

NCERT‚Äôs National Curriculum Framework 2005, though a school curriculum document, had profound implications for teacher development. It espoused a shift to constructivist, child-centered pedagogy, which required reorientation of both pre-service and in-service teacher education.

üí° Key Points:
  • NCF 2005 redefined the role of the teacher from an information-deliverer to a facilitator of learning.
  • It recommended that teacher education curricula be redesigned to give trainee teachers ample opportunities to observe children, engage in interactive learning, and reflect on the social-cultural context of education.
  • Trainees should learn to ‚Äúconstruct knowledge‚Äù along with learners, rather than just implementing given scripts.
  • The framework highlighted that a teacher must act as a mentor, facilitator, and co-learner ‚Äì organizing activities, guiding exploration, and addressing diverse needs, instead of purely lecturing.
  • It also pointed out that teacher training should integrate theory with practice, enabling teachers to contextualize theory in real classrooms and become reflective practitioners.
üìà Impact:
  • NCF 2005‚Äôs vision directly led to a nationwide push for training in new pedagogical techniques.
  • Through SSA, lakhs of in-service teachers were trained on NCF‚Äôs themes (group work, experiments, projects, etc.).
  • More structurally, NCF 2005 became a catalyst for NCTE to develop a fresh National Curriculum Framework for Teacher Education (NCFTE) 2009 that aligned teacher education with these constructivist principles.
  • Many colleges of education started revising their B.Ed syllabi (even before NCFTE) to include more practicum, school-based projects, and courses on child development and learning theories as envisaged by NCF 2005.
  • The framework thus helped bridge the gap between modern curriculum demands and teacher preparation, ultimately moving India‚Äôs teacher professional development discourse toward producing teachers who are innovative, reflective, and sensitive to child-centric learning.

Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act (RTE 2009)

The RTE Act 2009 made education a fundamental right for all 6‚Äì14 year-olds and mandated numerous reforms in elementary education. It had significant provisions regarding teachers‚Äô qualifications, training, and professional duties to ensure quality education.

üí° Key Points:
  • Required all teachers to meet minimum qualifications set by NCTE.
  • Untrained teachers had to obtain diplomas/degrees or face removal.
  • Introduced mandatory Teacher Eligibility Test (TET) for new teacher recruitment.
  • Restricted teacher deployment to non-teaching duties.
  • Enforced Pupil-Teacher Ratio (PTR), leading to new hires.
  • Triggered massive ODL-based training drive via NIOS post-2012 & 2017 amendments.
üìà Impact:
  • RTE 2009 put teacher qualifications and accountability on a legal footing.
  • States ramped up efforts to train or remove unqualified para-teachers.
  • The introduction of TET from 2011 onwards has significantly influenced pre-service training ‚Äì teacher education institutes had to better prepare their students for these tests, leading to greater focus on foundational knowledge and teaching aptitude.
  • The TET also set a common benchmark for teacher knowledge across India.
  • Moreover, RTE led to increased demand for teacher education as thousands sought to upgrade qualifications, and it spurred the growth of online/blended training programs.
  • Ensuring every school meets PTR norms meant continuous recruitment and induction training of new teachers, often under missions like SSA.
  • While challenges in compliance remained, by 2015 the proportion of untrained teachers had fallen sharply.
  • RTE thus enforced the idea that every teacher must be professionally trained and continually supported, reinforcing the importance of both pre-service qualifications and in-service development as part of the right to quality education.

NCTE Regulations (Two-Year B.Ed. and Curricular Reforms)

In late 2014, the NCTE, acting on the JVC recommendations, notified new Regulations that fundamentally altered teacher education programs. The headline change was the extension of B.Ed. and M.Ed. durations to two years each, effective from the 2015‚Äì16 academic session.

üí° Key Changes:
  • The two-year B.Ed. introduced four semesters instead of the old single year.
  • More school internship (often 16‚Äì20 weeks).
  • New courses on inclusive education, gender, ICT, etc.
  • D.El.Ed. and M.Ed. were also made two-year programs.
  • The Regulations included stricter infrastructure and faculty norms, plus a model curriculum for states to follow.
üìà Impact:
  • This restructuring addressed the longstanding criticism that one-year B.Ed was too rushed.
  • Early evidence suggested richer practical exposure and a better integration of theory and practice, however, variations in implementation existed.
  • Nonetheless, the 2014 Regulations marked a significant step toward improving teacher preparation quality, aligning Indian teacher education with global norms.

National Education Policy (NEP 2020)

After a gap of 34 years, India introduced a new National Education Policy in 2020. NEP 2020 devotes a full section to ‚ÄúTeachers‚Äù and ‚ÄúTeacher Education,‚Äù recognizing teachers as central to the education process and outlining ambitious reforms.

Key Points
  • By 2030, the minimum degree for teaching will be a 4-year integrated B.Ed offered only in multidisciplinary institutions.
  • Standalone teacher education colleges to be phased out.
  • All teachers to undergo minimum 50 hours of Continuous Professional Development (CPD) per year.
  • Launch of National Professional Standards for Teachers (NPST) and National Mission for Mentoring.
  • Emphasis on foundational literacy/numeracy, inclusive education, and educational technology.
üìà Impact:
  • NEP 2020 is in the early stages of implementation.
  • A new 4-Year Integrated Teacher Education Programme (ITEP) curriculum has been formulated, and pilot programs are underway in select universities.
  • Efforts to develop a NPST are ongoing, and continuous professional development is being scaled via online platforms like DIKSHA.
  • In the longer term, NEP 2020 envisions a consolidated, research-informed, and practice-oriented teacher education system.
